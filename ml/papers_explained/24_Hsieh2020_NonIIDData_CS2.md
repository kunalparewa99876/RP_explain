# 1. Research Context & Core Problem

## Exact Problem Formulation

- In decentralized learning (including federated learning and geo-distributed learning), training data lives on separate devices or data centers.
- These devices generate data in different real-world contexts, so the distribution of data labels varies significantly across devices. For example, cameras in Africa mostly capture zebras and lions, while cameras in Australia mostly capture kangaroos and koalas.
- This means the data across devices is **not identically distributed** (non-IID).
- The core question: **What happens to model accuracy when decentralized learning algorithms operate on non-IID (label-skewed) data partitions?**

## Why the Problem Exists

- Most decentralized learning algorithms were designed with an IID assumption -- they assume each device holds a representative random sample of the global dataset.
- In reality, data is generated by context (geography, user behavior, device type), so labels are naturally skewed.
- Communication between devices is expensive (limited bandwidth, privacy constraints like GDPR), so algorithms try to minimize communication -- but this makes the non-IID problem worse, because devices diverge more when they communicate less.

## Historical / Theoretical Gap

- Prior work (Gaia, FederatedAveraging, DeepGradientCompression) focused almost exclusively on **reducing communication**, either assuming IID data or running only very limited non-IID experiments.
- No prior study systematically evaluated the non-IID problem across multiple algorithms, models, applications, and degrees of skew.
- The interaction between batch normalization and non-IID data was unexplored.

## Limitations of Previous Approaches

- FederatedAveraging (McMahan et al., 2017) was shown to have problems with non-IID data, but only in narrow settings.
- Federated multi-task learning (Smith et al., 2017) trains local models per device rather than a single global model, which sidesteps the problem but does not solve it for the global model case.
- Proposed fixes like data sharing (Zhao et al., 2018) partially address the problem but do not generalize.

## Contribution Category

- **Empirical insight**: Systematic demonstration that non-IID labels are a fundamental problem.
- **System design**: SkewScout -- adaptive communication control based on measured data skew.
- **Practical observation**: Batch normalization breaks under non-IID settings; group normalization is a viable fix.

### Why This Paper Matters

This paper was the first to comprehensively prove that non-IID label skew is not a minor inconvenience but a **fundamental, pervasive problem** affecting all major decentralized learning algorithms. It shifted the research community's focus from "how to reduce communication" to "how to handle data heterogeneity." The findings about batch normalization's vulnerability and the SkewScout solution became foundational for subsequent federated learning research.

### Remaining Open Problems

- Non-IID data involves more than just label skew -- feature skew, concept drift, and conditional distribution shifts are also important but not studied here.
- SkewScout is tested only with Gaia; broader integration with other algorithms needs validation.
- Group normalization may not be a universal replacement for batch normalization in all DNN architectures and tasks.
- The study focuses on supervised classification; other learning paradigms (unsupervised, reinforcement learning, generative models) are unexplored.
- Scalability to hundreds or thousands of devices is not deeply evaluated.

# 2. Minimum Background Concepts

### Decentralized Learning

- **Definition**: Training a single ML model using data spread across multiple devices or locations, without collecting all data in one place.
- **Role in paper**: This is the entire setting being studied. The paper evaluates how decentralized learning algorithms behave when data across devices is not uniformly distributed.
- **Why authors needed it**: The motivation arises from real-world constraints -- privacy laws (GDPR), bandwidth limits, and data sovereignty make centralizing data impractical.

### IID vs Non-IID Data

- **IID (Independent and Identically Distributed)**: Each device holds a random sample from the same global distribution. Every device sees roughly the same types of data.
- **Non-IID**: Devices hold systematically different subsets of data. In this paper, specifically, different devices have different proportions of label classes (label skew).
- **Role in paper**: The entire study is about comparing algorithm performance under IID vs non-IID settings.

### Bulk Synchronous Parallel (BSP)

- **Definition**: A training protocol where all devices compute gradients, then share ALL updates with ALL other devices after every single training step.
- **Role in paper**: BSP is the gold standard baseline -- maximum communication, best model quality. All communication-efficient algorithms are compared against BSP.
- **Why authors needed it**: BSP represents the upper bound of model accuracy (since it uses full communication), so any accuracy loss under other algorithms is measured relative to BSP.

### Batch Normalization (BatchNorm)

- **Definition**: A technique that normalizes the inputs to each DNN layer using the mean and variance of the current mini-batch during training. During validation/testing, it uses running estimates of global mean and variance.
- **Role in paper**: Authors discover that BatchNorm is especially problematic under non-IID settings because different devices compute very different batch statistics, leading to a mismatch between training and validation behavior.
- **Why authors needed it**: BatchNorm is embedded in many popular architectures (ResNet, Inception, BN-LeNet), so understanding its failure under non-IID conditions is critical.

### Group Normalization (GroupNorm)

- **Definition**: An alternative to BatchNorm that computes mean and variance within groups of channels for each individual sample, rather than across a mini-batch.
- **Role in paper**: Proposed as a replacement for BatchNorm that avoids the non-IID problem because it does not depend on mini-batch statistics.
- **Why authors needed it**: To show that the BatchNorm-specific failure can be fixed by choosing a different normalization technique.

### Gaia, FederatedAveraging, DeepGradientCompression

- **Gaia**: Reduces communication by only sending updates whose magnitude exceeds a threshold. Small updates accumulate locally.
- **FederatedAveraging (FedAvg)**: Performs multiple local SGD steps on each device, then averages the resulting models at a central server.
- **DeepGradientCompression (DGC)**: Sends only the top-k% largest gradients; smaller gradients accumulate locally.
- **Role in paper**: These are the three decentralized learning algorithms evaluated. They represent three fundamentally different communication-reduction strategies (threshold-based, averaging-based, sparsity-based).

### Label Skew

- **Definition**: When the distribution of class labels P(y) differs across data partitions. For example, one device has mostly cats, another mostly dogs.
- **Role in paper**: This is the specific type of non-IID data studied.
- **Why authors needed it**: Label skew is the most commonly studied and most naturally occurring form of data heterogeneity in federated settings.

# 3. Mathematical / Theoretical Understanding Layer

This paper is primarily empirical, but it uses several important formalizations.

### Decentralized Learning Objective

**Intuition**: We want to train one model w that works well on ALL data across ALL K partitions.

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| w | Global model parameters |
| (x_i, y_i) | Training sample (feature, label) |
| P_k | Data partition k (data on device k) |
| K | Total number of partitions/devices |

**Assumption**: Standard decentralized learning assumes data in each P_k is drawn IID from the same distribution. This paper systematically violates that assumption.

### Non-IID Formalization

The paper uses the probability notation:
- **P_{P_i}(y) != P_{P_j}(y)** for different partitions P_i and P_j
- This means the distribution of labels is different across partitions.
- The authors control the degree of non-IID by specifying what percentage of the dataset is partitioned by labels vs randomly.

### SkewScout Optimization Objective

**Intuition**: Find the algorithm hyper-parameter theta that minimizes communication cost while keeping accuracy loss below a tolerable threshold.

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| theta | Hyper-parameter controlling communication (e.g., threshold T_0 for Gaia) |
| AL(theta) | Accuracy loss between local and remote partitions given theta |
| C(theta) | Communication cost given theta |
| CM | Communication cost of the full model |
| sigma_AL | Maximum tolerable accuracy loss threshold |
| lambda_AL | Weight for accuracy loss penalty (set to 50) |
| lambda_C | Weight for communication cost penalty (set to 1) |

**What it solves**: Balances the trade-off between communication efficiency and model accuracy under data skew.

**Practical interpretation**: SkewScout heavily prioritizes accuracy (lambda_AL = 50 >> lambda_C = 1), meaning it will increase communication significantly before it allows accuracy to drop.

**Limitation**: The optimization is solved via hill climbing, which may find local optima rather than the global optimum.

### Batch Normalization Divergence Metric

The authors measure how much the batch statistics diverge across partitions:

- **Divergence of mu_B** = ||mu_{B,P_0} - mu_{B,P_1}|| / ||AVG(mu_{B,P_0}, mu_{B,P_1})||
- In the IID setting: divergence is 1%-5%
- In the Non-IID setting: divergence is 6%-61%

**Practical interpretation**: This 6x-12x increase in divergence directly explains why BatchNorm fails under non-IID conditions -- the running mean/variance estimates become useless when training statistics vary so wildly across devices.

### Mathematical Insight Box

> **Key insight for researchers**: The non-IID problem in decentralized learning is fundamentally a **tug-of-war** problem. Each partition pulls the model toward its own local data distribution. Without sufficient communication, the model cannot find a compromise that works globally. The severity of this tug-of-war is directly proportional to the degree of label skew. Any solution must either (a) increase communication to resolve the tug-of-war more frequently, or (b) make the model architecture itself more robust to local distribution shifts (e.g., replacing BatchNorm with GroupNorm).

# 4. Proposed Method / Framework (MOST IMPORTANT)

The paper makes two main contributions: (1) a comprehensive empirical study, and (2) the SkewScout system. Below we detail both.

## 4.1 Empirical Study Design

### Overall Pipeline

1. Select diverse ML applications (image classification, face recognition)
2. Select diverse DNN architectures (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, LeNet, ResNet, center-loss face model)
3. Select diverse datasets (CIFAR-10, ImageNet, Flickr-Mammal, CASIA-WebFace)
4. Select three decentralized algorithms (Gaia, FedAvg, DGC) plus BSP baseline
5. Create both IID and Non-IID data partitions
6. Train all combinations and compare validation accuracy
7. Vary the degree of skew (20%, 40%, 60%, 80%, 100% non-IID) to study the impact

### Data Partitioning Method

- **100% Non-IID**: The entire dataset is partitioned by labels. With K=5 partitions and 10 classes (CIFAR-10), each partition gets exactly 2 classes.
- **Partial Non-IID (e.g., 40%)**: 40% of data is partitioned by labels, remaining 60% is distributed uniformly at random.
- **IID**: All data is distributed uniformly at random across partitions.

**Why authors did this**: This allows precise control of the skew degree, isolating the effect of label distribution from other factors.

**Weakness of this step**: Binary label partitioning (each label in exactly one partition) is an extreme case. Real-world skew is typically softer -- labels appear everywhere but in different proportions.

**How we could improve it**: Study continuous skew distributions (e.g., Dirichlet-based allocation), which more realistically model real-world heterogeneity.

### Flickr-Mammal Dataset Construction

1. Start with 48 mammal classes from Open Images V4
2. Use Flickr API to search for relevant geotagged images
3. Clean noisy search results using PNAS (pre-trained ImageNet classifier)
4. Result: 41 mammal classes, 736,005 pictures with geographic tags
5. Map geotags to continents using the M49 Standard

**Why authors did this**: To demonstrate that label skew is a real-world phenomenon, not just an artificial setting.

**Weakness of this step**: Flickr images have selection bias (photographers, tourists), so the dataset may not perfectly represent true geographic animal distributions.

**How we could improve it**: Use more curated wildlife datasets or combine multiple data sources to reduce photographer bias.

## 4.2 SkewScout System

### Overall Pipeline

1. **Model Traveling** (periodically): Send a copy of the local model from one partition to another.
2. **Remote Evaluation**: Evaluate the traveled model on a small subset of the remote partition's training data.
3. **Accuracy Loss Computation**: Compare remote accuracy to local accuracy to estimate divergence.
4. **Adaptive Hyper-parameter Tuning**: Use the accuracy loss to adjust the decentralized algorithm's communication hyper-parameter via optimization.
5. **Continue Training**: Resume normal decentralized training with the updated hyper-parameter.

### Component Details

#### Model Traveling

- Periodically (every 500 mini-batches), SkewScout sends a copy of one partition's model to another partition.
- The receiving partition evaluates this model using a small fraction of its local training data.
- This provides a direct measurement of how much the models have diverged.

**Why authors did this**: Direct accuracy comparison between partitions is the most intuitive and model-agnostic way to detect skew effects.

**Weakness**: Introduces communication overhead (sending a full model copy). Only measures pairwise divergence -- may miss complex multi-partition dynamics.

**How we could improve it**: Use lightweight model summaries (e.g., gradient statistics, feature distribution fingerprints) instead of sending the full model.

#### Adaptive Communication Control

- SkewScout adjusts hyper-parameters (e.g., Gaia's threshold T_0) based on the measured accuracy loss.
- Uses hill climbing to solve the optimization problem: minimize communication while keeping accuracy loss below sigma_AL = 5%.
- The search space is the set of possible hyper-parameter values for the specific algorithm.

**Why authors did this**: Different degrees of skew require different amounts of communication. A fixed setting wastes bandwidth when skew is mild and loses accuracy when skew is severe.

**Weakness**: Hill climbing may converge to suboptimal solutions. The approach is reactive (adjusts after detecting problems rather than proactively preventing them).

**How we could improve it**: Use more sophisticated optimization (Bayesian optimization, reinforcement learning) or combine with proactive methods like data augmentation.

### Simplified Pseudocode

```
SKEWSCOUT(algorithm, partitions, interval=500):
    theta = initial_hyperparameter
    
    FOR each training step t:
        // Normal decentralized training
        Run one step of algorithm with current theta
        
        IF t % interval == 0:
            // Model Traveling
            Send model from P_i to P_j
            local_acc = evaluate(model, P_i.subset)
            remote_acc = evaluate(model, P_j.subset)
            accuracy_loss = local_acc - remote_acc
            
            // Adaptive Communication Control
            theta = OPTIMIZE(
                minimize: lambda_C * C(theta) / CM + lambda_AL * max(0, AL(theta) - sigma_AL),
                using: hill_climbing
            )
    
    RETURN trained model
```

## 4.3 BatchNorm Fix: GroupNorm Replacement

### Pipeline

1. Identify all BatchNorm layers in the DNN.
2. Replace each BatchNorm layer with GroupNorm, using group size G_size tuned for the specific architecture.
3. Train normally -- GroupNorm computes normalization per-sample within channel groups, eliminating dependence on mini-batch statistics.

**Why authors did this**: BatchNorm's reliance on mini-batch statistics means non-IID partitions produce very different normalizations, causing the global model to fail. GroupNorm breaks this dependence.

**Weakness**: GroupNorm requires careful tuning of G_size per architecture. It may not match BatchNorm's performance in IID settings for all architectures.

**How we could improve it**: Develop adaptive normalization techniques that automatically switch between BatchNorm (when data is IID-like) and GroupNorm (when data is skewed).

# 5. Experimental Setup / Evaluation Design

## Dataset Characteristics

| Dataset | Task | Classes | Size | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| CIFAR-10 | Image Classification | 10 | 60K images | Standard benchmark |
| ImageNet | Image Classification | 1,000 | ~1.2M images | Large-scale benchmark |
| Flickr-Mammal | Image Classification | 41 | 736K images | Real-world geotagged, released by authors |
| CASIA-WebFace | Face Recognition | 10,575 identities | ~500K images | Training set |
| LFW | Face Verification | 5,749 identities | 13,233 images | Evaluation set |

## Experimental Protocol

- **Number of partitions**: K=5 for image classification (CIFAR-10, Flickr-Mammal), K=2 for face recognition.
- **IID baseline**: Data randomly shuffled across partitions.
- **Non-IID setting**: Data partitioned by labels (100% non-IID unless studying skew degree).
- **Training**: All training parameters tuned so BSP in IID matches reported accuracy from original papers. Same parameters used across all settings.
- **Convergence verification**: All experiments run until training/validation accuracy stops improving.

## Metrics Used and WHY

| Metric | Used For | Why This Metric |
|---|---|---|
| Top-1 Validation Accuracy | Image Classification | Standard metric; directly measures classification correctness |
| LFW Verification Accuracy | Face Recognition | Standard face verification metric; measures whether the model can determine if two face images are the same person |
| Accuracy Loss (relative to BSP-IID) | All experiments | Isolates the effect of non-IID data by comparing against the best possible scenario |
| Communication Savings (over BSP) | SkewScout evaluation | Directly quantifies the practical benefit of reducing communication |

## Baseline Selection Logic

- **BSP**: Upper bound on accuracy (maximum communication).
- **Oracle**: Lower bound on communication (unrealistically tests all hyper-parameters in advance to find the best one).
- **Three algorithms (Gaia, FedAvg, DGC)**: Represent three fundamentally different communication-reduction strategies, ensuring findings are not algorithm-specific.

## Hyper-parameter Reasoning

Hyper-parameters for each algorithm are selected by two criteria:
1. Must achieve same accuracy as BSP in the IID setting.
2. Must achieve similar communication savings across the three algorithms (~15x-20x).

Specific values:
- Gaia: T_0 = 10% (threshold for significance of updates)
- FedAvg: Iter_Local = 20 (local SGD steps before averaging)
- DGC: E_warm = 8 (epochs per warm-up sparsity stage)

## Hardware / Compute Assumptions

- Implemented in GPU parameter server system (GeePS) based on Caffe.
- Specific GPU details not emphasized (focus is on accuracy impact rather than wall-clock time).

### Experimental Reliability Analysis

**What is trustworthy**:
- Very broad coverage: 4 DNN architectures, 4 datasets, 3 algorithms, multiple skew levels.
- BSP baseline provides a solid reference point.
- Hyper-parameter sensitivity analysis confirms findings are not sensitive to specific settings.
- Real-world dataset (Flickr-Mammal) validates that the problem exists in practice, not just in synthetic setups.
- Code and datasets publicly released for reproducibility.

**What is questionable**:
- Extreme 100% non-IID setting (each label in exactly one partition) exaggerates the problem -- real-world skew is typically softer.
- Only K=5 and K=2 partitions studied in depth (appendices cover more, but primary results use few partitions).
- SkewScout is evaluated only with Gaia, not with FedAvg or DGC.
- No statistical significance tests (e.g., confidence intervals, repeated runs) are reported.
- Face recognition uses only K=2, which is a very limited setting.

# 6. Results & Findings Interpretation

## Main Outcomes

### Finding 1: Non-IID data is a pervasive problem

- ALL three decentralized learning algorithms suffer major accuracy loss in non-IID settings.
- Accuracy losses range from **3% to 74%** across different model-algorithm combinations.
- In the IID setting, these same algorithms retain BSP accuracy with 15x-20x communication savings.
- Some combinations completely diverge (DGC with GoogLeNet and ResNet20 fail to train at all).
- The problem persists across image classification AND face recognition.
- On the real-world Flickr-Mammal dataset, accuracy losses of 3.2%-5.5% occur even with naturally softer skew.

### Finding 2: Batch normalization is especially vulnerable

- Even BSP (full communication every step) loses 39% accuracy with ResNet20 in the non-IID setting.
- This happens because BatchNorm's mini-batch statistics (mean and variance) diverge 6x-12x more across partitions in non-IID vs IID.
- The mismatch between per-partition training statistics and global validation statistics breaks the model.
- This is NOT a communication problem -- more communication cannot fix it.
- Replacing BatchNorm with GroupNorm recovers BSP accuracy (79.2% vs 79.2% in IID).
- GroupNorm also dramatically helps decentralized algorithms (reducing additional accuracy loss by 10.7%-60.2%).

### Finding 3: Degree of skew determines difficulty

- Even 40% non-IID data causes 1.5%-3.0% accuracy loss.
- The gap between 80% and 20% non-IID can be as large as 7.4%.
- The relationship between skew degree and accuracy loss is roughly monotonic.

### Finding 4: SkewScout effectively adapts communication

- SkewScout achieves **9.6x to 34.1x** communication savings over BSP while retaining the same accuracy.
- More savings under milder skew (34.1x) vs high skew (9.6x) -- the system automatically tightens communication when needed.
- SkewScout is within **1.1x-1.5x** of the unrealistic Oracle baseline.

## Performance Trends

- The accuracy loss gap between IID and non-IID **increases** with more aggressive communication reduction.
- Models with batch normalization show a **discontinuous** degradation -- they fail catastrophically rather than degrading gradually.
- Face recognition (50% accuracy = random guessing) shows that non-IID can make models completely useless, even though validation labels are entirely different from training labels.

## Failure Cases

- DGC diverges even in IID settings for ResNet20, and fails more broadly in non-IID.
- Gaia and FedAvg achieve 50% on face recognition in non-IID (equivalent to random guessing).
- BatchNorm models cannot be fixed by simply increasing communication.

## Unexpected Observations

- Face recognition accuracy collapses despite validation labels being completely disjoint from training labels. This shows that non-IID training affects the learned feature representations, not just the classification layer.
- GroupNorm does not fully solve the problem for decentralized algorithms (still 8.7%-14.4% loss), only for BSP. The communication reduction problem remains even with correct normalization.

### Publishability Strength Check

**Publication-grade results**:
- The pervasiveness finding across 4 models, 3 algorithms, 4 datasets is very strong.
- The BatchNorm vulnerability is a novel, surprising, and practically important finding.
- The Flickr-Mammal real-world validation adds credibility.
- SkewScout's near-Oracle performance is compelling.

**Needs stronger validation**:
- SkewScout tested only with Gaia; integration with FedAvg and DGC would strengthen the claim of generality.
- No error bars or repeated runs reported.
- The 100% non-IID setting may overstate the problem for practical scenarios.

## Explicit Weaknesses

| # | Weakness | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Only label skew studied; feature skew, concept drift ignored | Findings may not generalize to all non-IID types |
| 2 | SkewScout evaluated only with Gaia | Generality claim is partially unsubstantiated |
| 3 | No statistical significance reporting | Cannot assess result reliability quantitatively |
| 4 | Extreme 100% non-IID as the primary experimental condition | May overstate severity of the problem |
| 5 | GroupNorm requires architecture-specific G_size tuning | Not a universal drop-in fix |
| 6 | Hill climbing for SkewScout optimization | May miss better hyper-parameter configurations |
| 7 | Small number of partitions (K=2 to K=5) | Scalability to realistic federated settings (hundreds of devices) unknown |
| 8 | Caffe-based implementation (dated framework) | Limits direct adoption; modern frameworks (PyTorch, TensorFlow) need reimplementation |

## Hidden Assumptions

| # | Assumption | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | All partitions participate in every round | Real federated learning has device dropout and partial participation |
| 2 | Partitions have roughly equal data sizes | Real-world data is often highly imbalanced across devices |
| 3 | Labels are the primary source of distribution difference | In practice, features, quality, noise levels also vary |
| 4 | The validation set is globally balanced | If validation set is also skewed, results would differ |
| 5 | Training parameters optimal for IID are also reasonable for non-IID | Learning rate, batch size, etc. might need separate tuning for non-IID |
| 6 | Communication cost is the primary bottleneck | Compute heterogeneity and straggler effects are not considered |
| 7 | Label partitioning with equal class counts per partition | Real-world skew rarely has such clean, balanced partitions |

# 10. Future Research Expansion Map

## Author-Suggested Future Work

- Extend the study to other forms of non-IID data (feature skew, conditional distribution shifts).
- Test GroupNorm as a BatchNorm replacement across more architectures and tasks.
- Integrate SkewScout with more decentralized learning algorithms.
- Study the problem at larger scale (more partitions, more devices).

## Missing Directions Not Addressed by Authors

- **Personalization vs globalization trade-off**: When should we train personalized local models vs a single global model under non-IID data?
- **Dynamic non-IID**: What happens when the data distribution at each device changes over time (concept drift)?
- **Heterogeneous compute**: How does compute heterogeneity interact with data heterogeneity?
- **Privacy implications**: Does SkewScout's model traveling leak sensitive information? How does it interact with differential privacy?
- **Fairness under non-IID**: Does non-IID data lead to unfair models that perform well for some devices but poorly for others?

## Modern Extensions (Post-2020)

- **FedProx** (Li et al., 2020): Adds a proximal term to handle statistical heterogeneity -- directly inspired by problems documented in this paper.
- **SCAFFOLD** (Karimireddy et al., 2020): Uses control variates to correct gradient drift under non-IID -- addresses the gradient divergence problem identified here.
- **FedBN** (Li et al., 2021): Keeps BatchNorm layers local (not aggregated), directly building on this paper's BatchNorm finding.
- **Per-FedAvg** and personalization approaches: Blend global and local models to handle non-IID -- operationalize the tug-of-war insight from this paper.

## Cross-Domain Combinations

- **Non-IID + differential privacy**: How does adding privacy noise interact with non-IID accuracy loss? Does privacy amplify or dampen the problem?
- **Non-IID + model compression**: Pruned or quantized models may be more or less sensitive to non-IID data.
- **Non-IID + continual learning**: Devices that continuously receive new data face both non-IID and catastrophic forgetting.

## LLM-Era / Emerging Extensions

- **Federated fine-tuning of LLMs**: With LoRA and adapter-based fine-tuning, how does non-IID data affect federated fine-tuning of large language models?
- **Federated instruction tuning**: Different users/organizations have different instruction datasets with different label distributions.
- **Normalization in transformers**: LayerNorm (used in transformers) may have different non-IID sensitivity than BatchNorm -- worthy of study in federated LLM settings.

# 11. How to Write a NEW Paper From This Work

### Reusable Elements

- **Experimental methodology**: The three-dimensional study design (algorithms x models x skew levels) is an excellent template for systematic empirical papers.
- **Real-world dataset construction**: Using geotagged data to demonstrate naturally occurring skew is a powerful validation strategy.
- **Controlled skew parameter**: The "X% non-IID" mechanism for controlling skew degree is widely reusable.
- **BSP as upper bound, Oracle as lower bound**: This baselining strategy cleanly frames any adaptive method.
- **Accuracy loss as a divergence metric**: Simple, intuitive, and directly meaningful.

### What MUST NOT Be Copied

- Do not replicate the exact same experimental conditions without adding something new.
- Do not claim the "pervasiveness" finding as novel -- it is now well-established.
- Do not simply re-run these experiments with a different framework (e.g., PyTorch) without new insights.
- The Flickr-Mammal dataset itself cannot be claimed as your contribution (it belongs to Hsieh et al.).

### How to Design a Novel Extension

1. **Pick a specific weakness from Section 8** (e.g., "only label skew studied").
2. **Formulate a concrete hypothesis** (e.g., "Feature skew causes different failure modes than label skew in decentralized learning").
3. **Design experiments that isolate the new variable** while keeping the rest of the methodology comparable.
4. **Propose a solution** that addresses the newly identified problem.
5. **Compare against baselines** including methods from this paper (GroupNorm, SkewScout).

### Minimum Publishable Contribution Checklist

- [ ] New type of non-IID data studied (not just label skew)
- [ ] OR new solution that outperforms SkewScout/GroupNorm
- [ ] OR new architecture finding (equivalent of the BatchNorm discovery)
- [ ] OR significantly larger scale (100+ devices, modern models)
- [ ] Experiments cover at least 2 algorithms and 2 datasets
- [ ] Comparison against at least FedAvg + one other algorithm
- [ ] Both IID and non-IID settings reported
- [ ] Ablation study showing each component's contribution
- [ ] Statistical significance (multiple runs, error bars)
- [ ] Code released for reproducibility

# 13. Publication Strategy Guide

## Suitable Conference/Journal Types

| Venue Type | Examples | Fit Level |
|---|---|---|
| Top ML conferences | ICML, NeurIPS, ICLR | High (original paper was at ICML 2020) |
| Systems + ML | MLSys, OSDI, NSDI, SoCC | High for system-level contributions |

| IEEE/ACM journals | TPAMI, TPDS, JSAC | For comprehensive extensions with theory |
| Applied ML venues | AAAI, IJCAI | For application-specific findings |

## Required Baseline Expectations

For a paper extending this work, reviewers will expect comparison against:
- **FedAvg** (McMahan et al., 2017) -- the most popular baseline
- **FedProx** (Li et al., 2020) -- the standard non-IID-aware algorithm
- **SCAFFOLD** (Karimireddy et al., 2020) -- the standard gradient correction method
- **SkewScout** (this paper) -- if your contribution is a system-level solution
- **BSP** -- as the accuracy upper bound

## Experimental Rigor Level

- **Minimum**: 2 datasets, 2 algorithms, 3 skew levels, 3 random seeds with error bars.
- **Competitive**: 3+ datasets (including one real-world), 3+ algorithms, 5+ skew levels, 5+ seeds, ablation study.
- **Top-tier**: All the above plus theoretical analysis, large-scale experiments (100+ devices), and code release.

## Common Rejection Reasons

1. **Insufficient baselines**: Not comparing against FedProx, SCAFFOLD, or other post-2020 methods.
2. **Only label skew**: Reviewers increasingly expect evaluation on multiple types of heterogeneity.
3. **No ablation**: Not showing which component of the proposed method provides the improvement.
4. **No error bars**: Single-run results are considered unreliable.
5. **Incremental improvement**: Must show clear, significant improvement over SCAFFOLD/FedProx, not just over vanilla FedAvg.
6. **Missing privacy analysis**: If claiming relevance to federated learning, privacy implications must be discussed.
7. **Outdated baselines**: Comparing only against pre-2020 methods.

## Increment Needed for Acceptance

- **Systems venue** (MLSys, OSDI): Need a working system with real deployment or large-scale simulation; empirical improvements of 2x+ on a meaningful metric.
- **ML venue** (ICML, NeurIPS): Need either (a) new theoretical understanding + supporting experiments, or (b) significant empirical improvements (1-3% on accuracy) with strong ablations and analysis.
- **Workshop**: New perspective or preliminary results are sufficient; partial evaluations accepted.

# 14. Researcher Quick Reference Tables

## Key Terminology Table

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Non-IID | Data partitions that are not identically distributed across devices |
| Label skew | Different devices have different proportions of data classes |
| BSP (Bulk Synchronous Parallel) | Full gradient synchronization after every training step |
| Gaia | Threshold-based communication reduction: only sends updates exceeding a magnitude threshold |
| FederatedAveraging (FedAvg) | Multiple local SGD steps followed by model averaging at server |
| DeepGradientCompression (DGC) | Top-k% gradient sparsification with warm-up schedule |
| BatchNorm | Normalizes layer inputs using mini-batch mean and variance |
| GroupNorm | Normalizes within channel groups per sample, independent of mini-batch |
| SkewScout | Adaptive communication control based on measured accuracy divergence between partitions |
| Model Traveling | Sending a model copy to a remote partition for evaluation |
| Accuracy Loss | Difference in model accuracy between local and remote partitions |
| X% Non-IID | X% of data is partitioned by labels; (100-X)% is randomly distributed |

## Important Equations Summary

| Equation | Purpose | Key Variables |
|---|---|---|
| P_{P_i}(y) != P_{P_j}(y) | Formal non-IID definition | P_i, P_j: different data partitions |
| BatchNorm divergence = \|\|mu_{B,P0} - mu_{B,P1}\|\| / \|\|AVG(mu_{B,P0}, mu_{B,P1})\|\| | Measures how much batch statistics diverge across partitions | mu_B: mini-batch mean per partition |
| SkewScout objective: min lambda_C * C(theta)/CM + lambda_AL * max(0, AL(theta) - sigma_AL) | Optimizes communication-accuracy trade-off | theta: algorithm hyper-parameter; sigma_AL: accuracy loss threshold |

## Parameter Meaning Table

| Parameter | Algorithm | Meaning | Typical Value |
|---|---|---|---|
| T_0 | Gaia | Initial significance threshold for updates | 10% (main experiments) |
| Iter_Local | FedAvg | Number of local SGD steps between aggregations | 20 |
| E_warm | DGC | Epochs per warm-up sparsity stage | 8 |
| K | All | Number of data partitions / devices | 2-5 |
| G_size | GroupNorm | Number of channels per normalization group | 2 (for BN-LeNet) |
| sigma_AL | SkewScout | Maximum tolerable accuracy loss | 5% |
| lambda_AL | SkewScout | Weight for accuracy loss in objective | 50 |
| lambda_C | SkewScout | Weight for communication cost in objective | 1 |

## Algorithm Flow Summary

### Gaia Flow
```
For each training step:
    Compute local gradients
    Accumulate updates for each weight w_j
    IF |accumulated_update| / |w_j| > threshold T_0:
        Send update to all partitions
        Reset accumulator
    ELSE:
        Keep accumulating locally
```

### FedAvg Flow
```
For each round:
    Server selects subset of partitions
    Each selected partition:
        Initialize with global model
        Run Iter_Local steps of local SGD
        Send updated model to server
    Server averages all received models
```

### SkewScout Flow
```
During training with any decentralized algorithm:
    Every 500 mini-batches:
        Send local model to remote partition (model traveling)
        Evaluate model on remote data subset
        Compute accuracy loss = local_acc - remote_acc
        Adjust algorithm hyper-parameter theta via hill climbing
        Goal: minimize communication while AL(theta) < sigma_AL
```

# 15. One-Page Master Summary Card

## Problem
Decentralized learning algorithms assume IID data across devices, but real-world data is non-IID (especially label-skewed). The impact of this mismatch was poorly understood.

## Idea
Conduct the first comprehensive empirical study of label skew across multiple algorithms, models, and datasets, then propose an adaptive system (SkewScout) that adjusts communication based on measured skew.

## Method
- Evaluated 3 decentralized algorithms (Gaia, FedAvg, DGC) + BSP across 4 DNN architectures, 4 datasets, and multiple skew levels.
- Discovered BatchNorm fails under non-IID even with BSP; GroupNorm fixes this.
- Built SkewScout: periodically moves models between partitions, measures accuracy divergence, and tunes communication hyper-parameters via hill climbing.

## Results
- 3%-74% accuracy loss across all algorithms in non-IID settings.
- BatchNorm causes 39% accuracy loss even with BSP on ResNet20.
- GroupNorm fully recovers BSP accuracy in non-IID.
- SkewScout achieves 9.6x-34.1x communication savings over BSP with no accuracy loss.

## Weakness
- Only label skew studied (not feature skew, concept drift).
- SkewScout tested only with Gaia.
- No statistical significance reported.
- Small number of partitions (K=2-5).

## Research Opportunity
- Extend to multi-dimensional non-IID (feature + label + quantity skew).
- Develop adaptive normalization that auto-switches between BatchNorm and GroupNorm.
- Scale SkewScout to hundreds of devices with modern frameworks.
- Combine skew-awareness with personalization techniques.

## Publishable Extension
"An adaptive normalization and communication framework for federated learning under multi-dimensional data heterogeneity" -- studies feature skew and label skew simultaneously, proposes a learned normalization strategy that adapts to detected heterogeneity type, and extends SkewScout-style communication control to FedAvg and SCAFFOLD with Bayesian optimization instead of hill climbing.
